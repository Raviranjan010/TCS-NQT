# Common Mistakes — Top 15 Candidate Errors Across All Sections

> These are **cross-section, strategic mistakes** — not topic-specific calculation errors (those are in the `mistakes.md` file inside each topic folder).

---

## Mistake 1 — Trusting Random Online "TCS NQT Pattern" PDFs

**What happens:** Candidates study for a 90-question/90-minute exam they found on a coaching site, walk in, and find a different structure.

**Why:** TCS changes the pattern across cycles. Many coaching sites copy old data without updating.

**Fix:** Always verify from your **official TCS iON dashboard and call letter**. Read the instructions screen at the start of the exam — it shows the actual structure for your session.

---

## Mistake 2 — Treating All Sections Equally

**What happens:** Candidate spends 60% of prep time on Verbal (their comfort zone) and 10% on Coding. They clear Verbal easily but fail the Coding section required for Digital/Prime.

**Fix:** Allocate prep time proportional to **difficulty × weightage**. Coding is hardest to improve last-minute and has the highest Prime-band impact. Give it the most time.

---

## Mistake 3 — Practising Without a Timer

**What happens:** Candidate can solve every practice problem... given unlimited time. In the actual exam, they run out of time with 8 questions unattempted.

**Fix:** After the first 2 days of concept learning, **all practice must be timed**. Start with 1.5× the target time, reduce to 1× within a week. This is non-negotiable.

---

## Mistake 4 — Blind Guessing When Stuck

**What happens:** Candidate marks a random option rather than skipping. With −0.33 negative marking, 3 wrong guesses cancel 1 correct answer.

**Fix:** Skip the question if you cannot eliminate at least 2 of 4 options. Come back later. If still unsure, use structured elimination — never random selection.

---

## Mistake 5 — Re-Reading the RC Passage for Every Question

**What happens:** Candidate reads the passage, answers Q1, re-reads for Q2, re-reads for Q3. Uses 20+ minutes on one passage.

**Fix:** Read the passage once with focus. Answer all questions from that passage using memory + targeted scanning for specific facts. Never do a full re-read per question.

---

## Mistake 6 — Writing Code That Only Handles the Sample Input

**What happens:** Candidate writes code that passes the 2 shown examples but fails on all hidden test cases (empty input, large numbers, duplicates, negative values).

**Fix:** After writing your solution, manually test: empty array, single element, all-same elements, maximum constraint input, negative numbers. These are the standard edge cases.

---

## Mistake 7 — Starting Coding From Scratch Without Planning

**What happens:** Candidate reads the problem and immediately starts typing. After 20 minutes, realizes the approach is wrong, starts over, runs out of time.

**Fix:** Spend the **first 3–5 minutes only reading and planning** — write the algorithm in comments before any code. This feels slow but prevents fatal restarts.

---

## Mistake 8 — Not Attempting the Coding Section at All

**What happens:** Candidate is not confident in coding, skips it entirely. They lose all Coding marks including easy test cases that a brute-force would pass.

**Fix:** Always submit something. Even a brute-force O(n²) solution that passes 3 of 10 test cases gives partial marks. An empty submission gives 0. Writing even a skeleton with correct output for trivial cases is better than nothing.

---

## Mistake 9 — Spending Too Long on One Hard Question

**What happens:** Candidate gets stuck on question 4 (which is difficult), spends 8 minutes on it, and rushes through or skips the next 10 easier questions.

**Fix:** Hard cap per question: **90 seconds for Quant/Reasoning, 60 seconds for Verbal**. When the timer in your head hits that limit → mark/flag, skip, move on. The next question might take 20 seconds.

---

## Mistake 10 — Changing Correct Answers During Review

**What happens:** Candidate finishes with 4 minutes left, reviews, second-guesses a correct answer, changes it, and loses the mark.

**Fix:** During review, **only change an answer if you have a clear, logical reason** — not just a vague feeling. Studies and exam experience consistently show that first instincts are right more often than second-guesses during review.

---

## Mistake 11 — Ignoring Negative Marking in Practice

**What happens:** Candidate practices on platforms that don't penalize wrong answers. They develop a habit of attempting everything regardless of confidence. In the actual exam, wrong guesses destroy their score.

**Fix:** Simulate negative marking in all practice. Use this rule: wrong answer = −1/3 of a mark. After each mock test, calculate your score both ways (with and without negatives) to see how guessing affected you.

---

## Mistake 12 — Not Reading the Full Question Statement

**What happens:** Candidate reads the first two lines of a question, identifies a keyword ("profit", "interest"), jumps to solving, picks a formula — and gets the wrong answer because the last line changed a critical condition.

**Fix:** Always read the complete question, including the last sentence. TCS NQT frequently places the key constraint (e.g., "loss instead of profit", "per annum vs per half-year") at the end of the question.

---

## Mistake 13 — Memorising Formulas Without Understanding Why They Work

**What happens:** Candidate memorizes the SI formula but can't adapt when TCS asks a variation (e.g., "amount doubles in 5 years, find time to triple").

**Fix:** For every formula, understand the derivation and the underlying concept. The `notes.md` and `formula.md` files in each topic folder explain the "why". Variations require conceptual understanding, not formula lookup.

---

## Mistake 14 — Leaving Email Writing Completely Unprepared

**What happens:** If the exam includes an Email Writing section, unprepared candidates write casual, grammatically incorrect emails and score poorly on a section that takes only 8–10 minutes to prepare.

**Fix:** Practice 5–10 formal email templates (request, apology, complaint, information sharing). Know the structure: Subject line → Salutation → Context (1–2 sentences) → Main Request/Content (2–3 sentences) → Closing → Signature. See [05_Email_Paragraph_Writing/](../05_Email_Paragraph_Writing/README.md).

---

## Mistake 15 — Underestimating Verbal Section for Ninja Band

**What happens:** Engineering students dismiss Verbal as "easy" and do zero prep. TCS Verbal includes inference-based RC, advanced fill-in-the-blanks with nuanced vocabulary, and error detection in compound sentences — none of which are trivial without practice.

**Fix:** Spend at least 15–20% of your prep time on Verbal. Focus on RC strategy and Fill in the Blanks — these are highest-yield topics. Do **not** spend excessive time on grammar rules you already intuitively know.
