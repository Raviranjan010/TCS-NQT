## ✏️ Mock Test Questions

Q1. The price of a machine depreciates by 10% in the first year, increases by 15% in the second year, and depreciates by 20% in the third year. If the initial cost of the machine was ₹2,50,000, what is its value at the end of 3 years?
A) ₹2,07,000  B) ₹1,95,000  C) ₹2,02,500  D) ₹2,12,000
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Successive percentage changes can be calculated using multiplying factors: $V_{final} = V_{initial} \times (1 + \frac{x}{100}) \times (1 + \frac{y}{100}) \times (1 + \frac{z}{100})$.
- **Derivation:** A change of $k\%$ changes a quantity $Q$ to $Q(1 + k/100)$. Repeating this successively yields the product of individual multipliers.
- **Calculation:** $V_{final} = 2,50,000 \times (1 - 0.10) \times (1 + 0.15) \times (1 - 0.20) = 2,50,000 \times 0.9 \times 1.15 \times 0.8 = 2,50,000 \times 0.72 \times 1.15 = 1,80,000 \times 1.15 = ₹2,07,000$.
- **Shortcut:** Combine the two easy multipliers first: $0.9 \times 0.8 = 0.72$. Multiply $2,50,000 \times 0.72 = 1,80,000$. Then add $15\%$ of $1,80,000$ (which is $18,000 + 9,000 = 27,000$) to get ₹2,07,000.
- **Variation & Trap:** Watch out for the order of appreciation and depreciation; although the order doesn't affect the final product (commutative property), students often apply appreciation as depreciation.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Percentage

Q2. A man spends 75% of his income. If his income increases by 20% and his expenditure increases by 15%, by what percentage do his savings increase?
A) 30%  B) 35%  C) 40%  D) 25%
Answer: B
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Income (I)} = \text{Expenditure (E)} + \text{Savings (S)}$. $\text{Savings Increase \%} = \frac{\Delta S}{S_{initial}} \times 100$.
- **Derivation:** Change in income must equal the sum of changes in expenditure and savings: $\Delta I = \Delta E + \Delta S$.
- **Calculation:** Let initial Income $= 100 \implies$ Expenditure $= 75$, Savings $= 25$. $\Delta I = 20\% \text{ of } 100 = 20$. $\Delta E = 15\% \text{ of } 75 = 11.25$. Therefore, $\Delta S = \Delta I - \Delta E = 20 - 11.25 = 8.75$. Savings Increase $\% = \frac{8.75}{25} \times 100 = 35\%$.
- **Shortcut:** Use weighted averages: $100\% \times (\Delta I) = 75\% \times (\Delta E) + 25\% \times (\Delta S) \implies 4 \times 20\% = 3 \times 15\% + 1 \times \Delta S \implies 80 = 45 + \Delta S \implies \Delta S = 35\%$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not calculate the new savings ($33.75$) and divide by new income; the denominator must be the initial savings ($25$).
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Percentage

Q3. In an examination, 65% of the students passed in Mathematics, 55% passed in Physics, and 40% passed in both. If 300 students failed in both subjects, what was the total number of students who appeared for the exam?
A) 1,200  B) 1,500  C) 1,600  D) 1,800
Answer: B
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Set Theory Principle: $n(M \cup P) = n(M) + n(P) - n(M \cap P)$.
- **Derivation:** Summing $n(M)$ and $n(P)$ counts the intersection twice, so we subtract $n(M \cap P)$ to find unique individuals who passed at least one exam.
- **Calculation:** $\text{Passed in at least one subject} = 65\% + 55\% - 40\% = 80\%$. Therefore, $\text{Failed in both subjects} = 100\% - 80\% = 20\%$. Given: $20\% \text{ of Total} = 300 \implies \text{Total} = 300 / 0.20 = 1,500$ students.
- **Shortcut:** Quickly draw/imagine Venn circles: Only Math $= 65 - 40 = 25\%$, Only Physics $= 55 - 40 = 15\%$, Both $= 40\%$. Total passed $= 25 + 15 + 40 = 80\%$. Failed $= 20\% \implies 1/5$ of total $= 300 \implies \text{Total} = 1500$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not add $65\% + 55\% = 120\%$ and assume the rest is failed; the overlapping "passed in both" category must be subtracted first.
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Easy
Topic: Percentage

Q4. The price of petrol is increased by 28%. A motorist wants to limit the increase in his expenditure on petrol to only 8%. By what percentage should he reduce his consumption of petrol?
A) 15.6%  B) 16.7%  C) 14.5%  D) 13.8%
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Expenditure (E)} = \text{Price (P)} \times \text{Consumption (C)}$. Thus, $C_{new} = \frac{E_{new}}{P_{new}}$.
- **Derivation:** Multiplier relationship: $1 + \frac{\% \Delta E}{100} = (1 + \frac{\% \Delta P}{100}) \times (1 + \frac{\% \Delta C}{100})$.
- **Calculation:** Let initial $P=100, C=100 \implies E=10,000$. New $P=128$, Target $E=10,800$. New $C = \frac{10,800}{128} = 84.375$. Reduction in $C = 100 - 84.375 = 15.625\% \approx 15.6\%$.
- **Shortcut:** $\text{Reduction \%} = \frac{\% \Delta P - \% \Delta E}{100 + \% \Delta P} \times 100 = \frac{28 - 8}{128} \times 100 = \frac{20}{128} \times 100 = 15.625\%$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Many students calculate the simple difference $28\% - 8\% = 20\%$ as the answer, forgetting that the base price has risen to $128$.
Time target: 75 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Percentage

Q5. A shopkeeper marks up his goods by 40% above the cost price and then allows a discount of 20% on the marked price. In addition, he uses a faulty balance which reads 1000g for 900g of goods. What is his overall profit percentage?
A) 24.4%  B) 23.3%  C) 22.2%  D) 25.5%
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Overall profit factor $= \text{Markup factor} \times \text{Discount factor} \times \text{Weight ratio}$.
- **Derivation:** Selling price (SP) per true unit of weight sold determines actual margin. Profit ratio $= \frac{\text{SP}}{\text{CP}} \times \frac{\text{Claimed weight}}{\text{Actual weight}}$.
- **Calculation:** Markup factor $= 1.40$, Discount factor $= 0.80$, Weight ratio $= \frac{1000}{900}$. Overall Ratio $= 1.40 \times 0.80 \times \frac{10}{9} = 1.12 \times \frac{10}{9} = \frac{11.2}{9} \approx 1.2444 \implies 24.44\%$ profit.
- **Shortcut:** Write as fractions: $\frac{140}{100} \text{ (markup)} \times \frac{80}{100} \text{ (discount)} \times \frac{10}{9} \text{ (cheating)} = \frac{7}{5} \times \frac{4}{5} \times \frac{10}{9} = \frac{56}{45}$. Gain $\% = \frac{56 - 45}{45} \times 100 = \frac{11}{45} \times 100 = 24.44\%$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not use the weight ratio as $900/1000$. Since the shopkeeper cheats by giving less, the profit multiplier must be $>1$ (i.e., $1000/900$).
Time target: 75 seconds
Difficulty: Hard
Topic: Profit & Loss

Q6. An article is sold at a gain of 12%. Had it been purchased for 10% less and sold for ₹60 more, the profit would have been 30%. Find the original cost price of the article.
A) ₹1,200  B) ₹1,400  C) ₹1,500  D) ₹1,600
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Equate the two scenarios: $\text{New SP} - \text{Original SP} = \text{Change in price}$.
- **Derivation:** Initial: $\text{CP}_1 = 100x \implies \text{SP}_1 = 112x$. New: $\text{CP}_2 = 90x \implies \text{SP}_2 = 90x \times 1.30 = 117x$.
- **Calculation:** Given $\text{SP}_2 - \text{SP}_1 = 117x - 112x = 5x = 60 \implies x = 12$. Original $\text{CP} = 100x = 100 \times 12 = ₹1,200$.
- **Shortcut:** A $10\%$ reduction in CP reduces cost to $90\%$. A $30\%$ profit on this new cost yields $90 \times 1.3 = 117\%$ of original CP. The gain percentage jumps from $112\%$ to $117\%$ (a $5\%$ increase of CP), which corresponds to ₹60. Therefore, $100\% \text{ CP} = 60 \times 20 = ₹1,200$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Ensure to apply the $30\%$ profit rate to the *new* cost price ($90x$), not the original cost price ($100x$).
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Profit & Loss

Q7. A dealer marks his goods 30% above the cost price. He sells 60% of the goods at the marked price and the rest at a discount of 20% on the marked price. What is his overall gain percentage?
A) 18.4%  B) 19.6%  C) 21.2%  D) 20.8%
Answer: B
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Weighted profit multiplier: $\text{Overall Factor} = p \cdot M + (1 - p) \cdot M \cdot (1 - D)$, where $p$ is the fraction sold at full marked price, $M$ is marked price multiplier, and $D$ is discount rate.
- **Derivation:** Total Revenue divided by Total Cost: $\frac{\text{Revenue}}{\text{Cost}} = \frac{0.6 \cdot Q \cdot 1.3 \cdot CP + 0.4 \cdot Q \cdot 1.3 \cdot CP \cdot 0.8}{Q \cdot CP}$.
- **Calculation:** Let Total Cost $= 100$. $\text{Revenue} = (0.6 \times 130) + (0.4 \times 130 \times 0.8) = 78 + (0.4 \times 104) = 78 + 41.6 = 119.6$. Overall Gain $\% = 119.6 - 100 = 19.6\%$.
- **Shortcut:** Net multiplier $= 1.3 \times [0.6 + 0.4 \times 0.8] = 1.3 \times [0.6 + 0.32] = 1.3 \times 0.92 = 1.196 \implies 19.6\%$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not calculate discount as $30\% - 20\% = 10\%$ markup on the remaining goods; the discount is applied to the marked price ($130\%$), not the cost price.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Profit & Loss

Q8. The difference between the compound interest (compounded annually) and simple interest on a sum of money at 10% per annum for 3 years is ₹620. Find the principal sum.
A) ₹18,000  B) ₹20,000  C) ₹22,000  D) ₹25,000
Answer: B
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** For 3 years, $\text{Difference (D)} = P \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2 \left(\frac{300 + R}{100}\right)$.
- **Derivation:** $\text{CI} = P[(1+r)^3 - 1] = P[3r + 3r^2 + r^3]$. $\text{SI} = 3Pr$. Difference $\text{CI} - \text{SI} = P(3r^2 + r^3) = P r^2(3+r)$, where $r = R/100$.
- **Calculation:** $620 = P \left(\frac{10}{100}\right)^2 \left(\frac{310}{100}\right) \implies 620 = P \left(\frac{1}{100}\right) \left(\frac{310}{100}\right) \implies 620 = P \left(\frac{310}{10000}\right) \implies P = \frac{620 \times 10000}{310} = 20,000$.
- **Shortcut:** For $10\%$, $\text{SI} = 30\%$, $\text{CI} = 33.1\%$ (using successive growth $10 \rightarrow 21 \rightarrow 33.1$). Difference $= 3.1\%$. If $3.1\% \text{ of } P = 620 \implies 1\% \text{ of } P = 200 \implies P = ₹20,000$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not use the 2-year formula $D = P(R/100)^2$ by mistake; ensure the 3-year term factor $(\frac{300+R}{100})$ is multiplied.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Simple & Compound Interest

Q9. Find the compound interest on a sum of ₹16,000 for 1 year at 20% per annum, if the interest is compounded half-yearly.
A) ₹3,360  B) ₹3,200  C) ₹3,440  D) ₹3,520
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** For half-yearly compounding, $A = P(1 + \frac{R/2}{100})^{2n}$, where $n$ is years.
- **Derivation:** Interest is calculated and added twice a year, so the nominal annual rate is halved ($R_{half} = R/2$) and the compounding periods are doubled ($2n$).
- **Calculation:** $P = 16,000$, Rate $= 20\%/2 = 10\%$ per half-year, periods $= 2$. $A = 16,000 \times (1 + \frac{10}{100})^2 = 16,000 \times 1.21 = 19,360$. $\text{CI} = 19,360 - 16,000 = ₹3,360$.
- **Shortcut:** The effective interest rate for two cycles of $10\%$ is $10 + 10 + \frac{10 \times 10}{100} = 21\%$. $\text{CI} = 21\% \text{ of } 16,000 = 0.21 \times 16,000 = ₹3,360$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Beware: do not compound quarterly unless stated; half-yearly means divide the annual rate by 2 and multiply years by 2.
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Simple & Compound Interest

Q10. A, B, and C enter into a partnership. A invests ₹24,000 for 8 months, B invests ₹32,000 for 6 months, and C invests ₹40,000 for 6 months. If the total annual profit is ₹26,000, find the share of C.
A) ₹9,000  B) ₹10,000  C) ₹8,000  D) ₹12,000
Answer: B
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Profit Share Ratio $= (I_A \times T_A) : (I_B \times T_B) : (I_C \times T_C)$.
- **Derivation:** The return on investment is directly proportional to both the amount invested ($I$) and the time ($T$) for which it is invested.
- **Calculation:** Profit Ratio $= (24,000 \times 8) : (32,000 \times 6) : (40,000 \times 6) = 192 : 192 : 240$. Dividing all terms by 48 gives the simplified ratio $4 : 4 : 5$. Total parts $= 4 + 4 + 5 = 13$. C's share $= \frac{5}{13} \times 26,000 = ₹10,000$.
- **Shortcut:** Cancel out common thousands (24, 32, 40) $\rightarrow$ divide by 8 to get investments as 3, 4, 5. Multiply by times: $3 \times 8 : 4 \times 6 : 5 \times 6 = 24 : 24 : 30 = 4:4:5$. Share $= \frac{5}{13} \times 26,000 = 10,000$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not distribute profits purely based on investment ratios (24:32:40) without multiplying by their respective investment durations.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Ratio & Proportion

Q11. A vessel contains a mixture of milk and water in the ratio 7:5. When 9 liters of this mixture is drawn off and replaced with 9 liters of water, the ratio of milk to water becomes 7:9. How many liters of milk was contained in the vessel initially?
A) 21 liters  B) 27 liters  C) 24 liters  D) 18 liters
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Let initial mixture be $7x$ milk and $5x$ water.
- **Derivation:** Drawing off mixture does not change the ratio of components in the vessel. The changes occur only when pure water is added to replace the volume.
- **Calculation:**
  - Let remaining mixture after drawing off 9L have Milk $= 7y$ and Water $= 5y$.
  - Adding 9L of water makes the new ratio of Milk : Water $= 7y : (5y + 9) = 7 : 9 \implies \frac{7y}{5y+9} = \frac{7}{9} \implies y = 2.25$.
  - Remaining volume $= 12y = 12 \times 2.25 = 27$ liters.
  - Since 9L was drawn off, initial volume $= 27 + 9 = 36$ liters.
  - Initial Milk $= \frac{7}{12} \times 36 = 21$ liters.
- **Shortcut:** Milk quantity remains constant after drawing off. Ratio shifts: $7:5 \rightarrow 7:9$. The increase in water is $+4$ parts $= 9$ liters $\implies 1$ part $= 2.25$ liters. Total initial mixture $= 7+5=12$ parts $+ 9\text{L drawn off} = 12 \times 2.25 + 9 = 36$ liters. Initial Milk $= \frac{7}{12} \times 36 = 21$ liters.
- **Variation & Trap:** Make sure to find the *initial* milk quantity ($21$ liters), not the quantity of milk *after* the replacement ($21 - 5.25 = 15.75$ liters).
Time target: 70 seconds
Difficulty: Hard
Topic: Ratio & Proportion

Q12. If $x$ is the third proportional to 8 and 20, and $y$ is the fourth proportional to 3, 5, and 24, find the value of $2x + 3y$.
A) 220  B) 210  C) 200  D) 190
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Third proportional $x$ of $a$ and $b$ satisfies $a : b = b : x \implies x = \frac{b^2}{a}$. Fourth proportional $y$ of $a, b, c$ satisfies $a : b = c : y \implies y = \frac{bc}{a}$.
- **Derivation:** Proportion implies equality of ratios: $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{b}{x}$ and $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{y}$.
- **Calculation:**
  - $x = \frac{20^2}{8} = \frac{400}{8} = 50$.
  - $y = \frac{5 \times 24}{3} = 40$.
  - Value $= 2x + 3y = 2(50) + 3(40) = 100 + 120 = 220$.
- **Shortcut:** For $x$: $8 \times 2.5 = 20 \implies 20 \times 2.5 = 50$. For $y$: $3 \times 8 = 24 \implies 5 \times 8 = 40$. Thus, $2(50) + 3(40) = 220$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not confuse third proportional with mean proportional ($\sqrt{ab}$). For 8 and 20, mean proportional is $\sqrt{160} \approx 12.6$, whereas third proportional is $50$.
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Easy
Topic: Ratio & Proportion

Q13. A is twice as efficient as B, and B is thrice as efficient as C. If they together can complete a work in 12 days, in how many days can A alone complete the same work?
A) 20 days  B) 18 days  C) 24 days  D) 15 days
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Total Work} = \text{Combined Efficiency} \times \text{Days}$. $\text{Time}_A = \frac{\text{Total Work}}{\text{Efficiency}_A}$.
- **Derivation:** Work rate is additive. Rate $= R_A + R_B + R_C$. Time taken is inversely proportional to work rate.
- **Calculation:**
  - Let C's efficiency $= 1$ unit/day $\implies$ B's efficiency $= 3$ units/day $\implies$ A's efficiency $= 2 \times 3 = 6$ units/day.
  - Combined efficiency $= 6 + 3 + 1 = 10$ units/day.
  - Total Work $= 10 \times 12 = 120$ units.
  - Time for A alone $= \frac{120}{6} = 20$ days.
- **Shortcut:** Ratio of efficiency $A:B:C = 6:3:1$. Total parts $= 10$. A's share of efficiency $= 6/10 = 3/5$. Since time is inversely proportional to efficiency, $\text{Time}_A = 12 \times \frac{10}{6} = 20$ days.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not average their individual rates. Efficiency is direct, but time is inverse; hence, work units are the safest route.
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Time & Work

Q14. A and B working separately can complete a piece of work in 9 days and 12 days respectively. If they work for a day alternately, with A beginning the work, in how many days will the work be completed?
A) $10 \frac{1}{4}$ days  B) $10 \frac{1}{3}$ days  C) $10 \frac{1}{2}$ days  D) $9 \frac{3}{4}$ days
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Use LCM to find total work units, then compute work done per 2-day cycle.
- **Derivation:** Working alternately means A works on odd days and B works on even days. We accumulate work in blocks of 2 days.
- **Calculation:**
  - Let Total Work $= \text{LCM}(9, 12) = 36$ units.
  - A's rate $= 36 / 9 = 4$ units/day. B's rate $= 36 / 12 = 3$ units/day.
  - 1 cycle (2 days) $= 4 + 3 = 7$ units of work.
  - In 5 cycles (10 days), work done $= 5 \times 7 = 35$ units.
  - Remaining work $= 36 - 35 = 1$ unit.
  - On Day 11 (A's turn), time taken to complete 1 unit $= \frac{1}{4}$ day.
  - Total time $= 10 + \frac{1}{4} = 10 \frac{1}{4}$ days.
- **Shortcut:** Cycle rate $= 7$. $36 / 7 = 5$ full cycles $+ 1$ remainder. 5 cycles $= 10$ days. Remainder $1$ is completed by A (rate 4) in $1/4$ day. Total $= 10.25$ days.
- **Variation & Trap:** If B starts the work instead of A, the 11th day work is done by B (rate 3), taking $1/3$ day. Read the starting person carefully!
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Time & Work

Q15. 4 men and 6 women can complete a work in 8 days, while 3 men and 7 women can complete it in 10 days. In how many days can 10 women complete the work?
A) 40 days  B) 35 days  C) 30 days  D) 25 days
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Equate work equations: $W = (n_1 M + n_2 W) \times D_1 = (n_3 M + n_4 W) \times D_2$.
- **Derivation:** Total work is constant and is the product of total daily capacity (men and women combined) and the number of days.
- **Calculation:**
  - $(4M + 6W) \times 8 = (3M + 7W) \times 10 \implies 32M + 48W = 30M + 70W$.
  - $2M = 22W \implies 1M = 11W$.
  - Total Work in terms of women $= (4(11W) + 6W) \times 8 = 50W \times 8 = 400\text{ woman-days}$.
  - Days for 10 women $= \frac{400\text{ woman-days}}{10\text{ women}} = 40$ days.
- **Shortcut:** Find the ratio of efficiency first: $2M = 22W \implies M:W = 11:1$. Total work $= (4 \times 11 + 6 \times 1) \times 8 = 50 \times 8 = 400$. Time for 10 women (rate 1 each) $= 400 / 10 = 40$ days.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not add rates directly without finding the conversion ratio between men and women's daily outputs.
Time target: 75 seconds
Difficulty: Hard
Topic: Time & Work

Q16. Two trains of lengths 140 m and 160 m respectively are running on parallel tracks. If they run in opposite directions with speeds of 40 km/h and 50 km/h, how long will they take to cross each other completely from the moment they meet?
A) 12 seconds  B) 10 seconds  C) 15 seconds  D) 8 seconds
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Relative Speed}}$.
- **Derivation:** To cross each other, the front of the trains must cover a combined distance equal to the sum of their lengths. In opposite directions, their velocities add up.
- **Calculation:**
  - Total Distance $= 140 + 160 = 300$ m.
  - Relative Speed $= 40 + 50 = 90$ km/h.
  - Convert speed to m/s: $90 \times \frac{5}{18} = 25$ m/s.
  - Time $= \frac{300}{25} = 12$ seconds.
- **Shortcut:** Multiply relative speed by $5/18$ immediately: $90 \rightarrow 25$ m/s. Divide $300 / 25 = 12$s mentally.
- **Variation & Trap:** If the trains run in the *same* direction, relative speed is the difference: $50 - 40 = 10$ km/h $= \frac{25}{9}$ m/s, taking $300 \times \frac{9}{25} = 108$ seconds.
Time target: 50 seconds
Difficulty: Easy
Topic: Time, Speed & Distance

Q17. A person travels a distance of 300 km. He covers the first 120 km by train at a speed of 60 km/h, the next 150 km by car at 75 km/h, and the remaining distance by bus at 30 km/h. What is his average speed for the entire journey?
A) 60 km/h  B) 58 km/h  C) 62.5 km/h  D) 55 km/h
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$.
- **Derivation:** Average speed is defined as the uniform speed that covers the total distance in the same total time: $S_{avg} = \frac{d_1 + d_2 + d_3}{t_1 + t_2 + t_3}$.
- **Calculation:**
  - Total Distance $= 300$ km. Remaining distance $= 300 - (120 + 150) = 30$ km.
  - Time for train ($t_1$) $= 120 / 60 = 2$ hours.
  - Time for car ($t_2$) $= 150 / 75 = 2$ hours.
  - Time for bus ($t_3$) $= 30 / 30 = 1$ hour.
  - Total Time $= 2 + 2 + 1 = 5$ hours.
  - Average Speed $= 300 / 5 = 60$ km/h.
- **Shortcut:** Sum the hours directly: $2 + 2 + 1 = 5$. Total distance $300$. Average Speed $= 300/5 = 60$ km/h.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not calculate the simple average of the speeds: $\frac{60+75+30}{3} = 55$ km/h; this is incorrect because different times were spent at each speed.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Time, Speed & Distance

Q18. A boatman can row a certain distance downstream in 4 hours, and can row the same distance upstream in 6 hours. If the speed of the stream is 2 km/h, what is the speed of the boat in still water?
A) 10 km/h  B) 12 km/h  C) 8 km/h  D) 15 km/h
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Distance downstream $=$ Distance upstream $\implies (v + s) \times t_{down} = (v - s) \times t_{up}$.
- **Derivation:** Downstream speed is boat speed ($v$) plus stream speed ($s$). Upstream speed is boat speed ($v$) minus stream speed ($s$).
- **Calculation:**
  - Let boat speed in still water be $v$. Stream speed $s = 2$.
  - $(v + 2) \times 4 = (v - 2) \times 6 \implies 4v + 8 = 6v - 12 \implies 2v = 20 \implies v = 10$ km/h.
- **Shortcut:** Use ratio component: $\frac{v}{s} = \frac{t_{up} + t_{down}}{t_{up} - t_{down}} = \frac{6+4}{6-4} = \frac{10}{2} = 5$. Since $s = 2$, $v = 5 \times 2 = 10$ km/h.
- **Variation & Trap:** Ensure to keep the terms straight: downstream is faster (shorter time) and upstream is slower (longer time).
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Time, Speed & Distance

Q19. The average score of 40 students in a class is 72. Later it was found that the scores of two students were misread as 68 and 84 instead of their actual scores of 86 and 46 respectively. What is the correct average score of the class?
A) 71.5  B) 72.5  C) 73.0  D) 71.0
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $\text{Correct Average} = \text{Incorrect Average} + \frac{\text{Net Deviation}}{\text{Number of observations}}$.
- **Derivation:** Sum of elements $=$ Average $\times$ Count. Adjusting the sum by adding correct values and subtracting incorrect ones yields the correct average.
- **Calculation:**
  - Actual values $= 86 + 46 = 132$.
  - Incorrect misread values $= 68 + 84 = 152$.
  - Net Deviation $= 132 - 152 = -20$.
  - Average Change $= \frac{-20}{40} = -0.5$.
  - Correct Average $= 72 - 0.5 = 71.5$.
- **Shortcut:** Calculate error deviation: $(86 - 68) + (46 - 84) = 18 - 38 = -20$. Divide by total students: $-20 / 40 = -0.5$. Deduct from original average: $72 - 0.5 = 71.5$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not add the deviation $+0.5$; ensure the sign of the net deviation is correct (since actual sum $132$ is less than misread sum $152$, average must decrease).

Q20. The average of 7 consecutive odd numbers is 27. If the next 3 consecutive odd numbers are also included, what will be the new average of all the numbers?
A) 30  B) 29  C) 31  D) 28
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** The average of an arithmetic progression (AP) is its middle term: $\text{Avg} = \frac{\text{First Term} + \text{Last Term}}{2}$.
- **Derivation:** For 7 consecutive odd numbers, the 4th term is the middle term $= 27$. The numbers are $21, 23, 25, 27, 29, 31, 33$.
- **Calculation:**
  - Next 3 consecutive odd numbers: $35, 37, 39$.
  - Total numbers $= 10$. The numbers are $21, 23, 25, 27, 29, 31, 33, 35, 37, 39$.
  - New average $= \frac{21 + 39}{2} = \frac{60}{2} = 30$.
- **Shortcut:** If we add $k$ terms to a consecutive sequence with common difference $d$, the average increases by $\frac{k \times d}{2}$. Here $k=3$ terms added, and $d=2$ (since they are odd numbers). Average increases by $\frac{3 \times 2}{2} = 3$. New Average $= 27 + 3 = 30$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Note that consecutive numbers have a difference of 1, whereas consecutive *odd* or *even* numbers have a difference of 2.
Time target: 45 seconds
Difficulty: Easy
Topic: Averages

Q21. In how many different ways can the letters of the word "CORPORATION" be arranged so that all the vowels always come together?
A) 50,400  B) 25,200  C) 120,960  D) 40,320
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Treat grouped items as a single unit: $\text{Total permutations} = \frac{(\text{Consonants} + 1\text{ group})!}{\text{Repeats}} \times \frac{(\text{Vowels})!}{\text{Vowel Repeats}}$.
- **Derivation:** Grouping vowels makes them act as one block. Within that block, they can rearrange among themselves. We divide by factorial of repeat counts to discount identical arrangements.
- **Calculation:**
  - Consonants: C, R, P, R, T, N (6 consonants; R repeats 2 times).
  - Vowels: O, O, A, I, O (5 vowels; O repeats 3 times).
  - Total units to arrange $= 6 + 1 = 7$ units.
  - Ways to arrange units $= \frac{7!}{2!} = 2520$.
  - Ways to arrange vowels inside group $= \frac{5!}{3!} = 20$.
  - Total Arrangements $= 2520 \times 20 = 50,400$ ways.
- **Shortcut:** Write factorials directly: $\frac{7! \times 5!}{2! \times 3!} = \frac{5040 \times 120}{2 \times 6} = \frac{5040 \times 120}{12} = 5040 \times 10 = 50,400$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Make sure not to miss the repeat counts of R (in consonants) and O (in vowels); failing to divide by $2!$ and $3!$ is a common error.
Time target: 75 seconds
Difficulty: Hard
Topic: Permutations & Combinations

Q22. A committee of 5 members is to be formed from a group of 6 men and 4 women. In how many ways can this be done if the committee must contain at least 2 women?
A) 186  B) 192  C) 210  D) 150
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Sum combinations across valid cases: $\text{Total Ways} = \sum {^{W_{total}}C_{w_i}} \times {^{M_{total}}C_{m_i}}$.
- **Derivation:** We select $w_i$ women and $5-w_i$ men, where $w_i \ge 2$.
- **Calculation:**
  - Case 1: 2 Women, 3 Men $\implies ^4C_2 \times ^6C_3 = 6 \times 20 = 120$ ways.
  - Case 2: 3 Women, 2 Men $\implies ^4C_3 \times ^6C_2 = 4 \times 15 = 60$ ways.
  - Case 3: 4 Women, 1 Man $\implies ^4C_4 \times ^6C_1 = 1 \times 6 = 6$ ways.
  - Total Ways $= 120 + 60 + 6 = 186$ ways.
- **Shortcut:** Calculate total possible committees without constraints ($^{10}C_5 = 252$) and subtract invalid cases (0 women or 1 woman):
  - 0 women: $^{6}C_5 = 6$.
  - 1 woman: $^4C_1 \times ^6C_4 = 4 \times 15 = 60$.
  - Total valid $= 252 - (6 + 60) = 252 - 66 = 186$.
- **Variation & Trap:** "At least 2" includes up to 4 women (since there are only 4 women in total). Do not try to pick 5 women as it is physically impossible.
Time target: 70 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Permutations & Combinations

Q23. Three unbiased coins are tossed simultaneously. What is the probability of getting at most two heads?
A) 7/8  B) 3/4  C) 5/8  D) 1/2
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** Probability $P(A) = 1 - P(A')$, where $A'$ is the complement event.
- **Derivation:** The sum of probabilities of all outcomes in a sample space is 1. The complement of "at most 2 heads" is "exactly 3 heads".
- **Calculation:**
  - Total outcomes when tossing 3 coins $= 2^3 = 8$.
  - Outcome with 3 heads $\{HHH\} = 1$ event.
  - $P(\text{exactly 3 heads}) = 1/8$.
  - $P(\text{at most 2 heads}) = 1 - 1/8 = 7/8$.
- **Shortcut:** List elements: out of 8 outcomes, only 1 outcome ($HHH$) violates the condition. Favorable $= 8 - 1 = 7$. Probability $= 7/8$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Read "at most" and "at least" carefully. "At most two heads" means $\le 2$ heads, which includes 0, 1, or 2 heads.
Time target: 40 seconds
Difficulty: Easy
Topic: Probability

Q24. A bag contains 5 red, 4 blue, and 3 green balls. If three balls are drawn at random without replacement, what is the probability that they are of three different colors?
A) 3/11  B) 4/11  C) 6/11  D) 2/11
Answer: A
Solution:
- **Approach & Formula:** $P(\text{all different}) = \frac{n(\text{favorable})}{n(\text{total})} = \frac{^R C_1 \times ^B C_1 \times ^G C_1}{^{Total} C_3}$.
- **Derivation:** We draw one ball of each color. Favorable combinations is the product of selecting 1 ball from each group. Total combinations is selecting 3 from all.
- **Calculation:**
  - Total balls $= 5 + 4 + 3 = 12$.
  - Total ways to draw 3 balls $= ^{12}C_3 = \frac{12 \times 11 \times 10}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = 220$.
  - Favorable ways (1 Red, 1 Blue, 1 Green) $= ^5C_1 \times ^4C_1 \times ^3C_1 = 5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$.
  - Probability $= \frac{60}{220} = \frac{3}{11}$.
- **Shortcut:** Using sequential probability: $3! \times P(R \cap B \cap G) = 6 \times (\frac{5}{12} \times \frac{4}{11} \times \frac{3}{10}) = 6 \times \frac{60}{1320} = 6 \times \frac{1}{22} = \frac{3}{11}$.
- **Variation & Trap:** Do not forget to multiply by $3!$ (or 6) if using sequential probabilities, as the order of drawing (e.g. RBG, RGB, BRG, etc.) is not fixed.
Time target: 60 seconds
Difficulty: Medium
Topic: Probability

## 🚨 5 Exam-Day Numerical Section Tips

1. **Leverage Option Clues (Unit Digits & Divisibility):** Do not calculate full values immediately. Eliminate options by performing divisibility checks (e.g., sum digits to check for 9, alternating sum for 11) or checking unit digits of final multiplications.
2. **Successive Growth Multiplier Trick:** For successive increases/decreases, use multiplying decimals ($0.9$ for $10\%$ drop, $1.15$ for $15\%$ rise). Combine them first before multiplying by the base quantity.
3. **LCM-based Work Modeling:** Always convert work rates to integers using LCM of the given days/hours. This eliminates complex fraction addition and keeps your scratchpad calculations clean.
4. **Weighted Averages for Mixture/Replacements:** When replacing or mixing items, use the deviation method: $\text{New Average} = \text{Old Average} + \frac{\text{Net Deviation}}{\text{Count}}$. This saves you from performing huge aggregations.
5. **Boats/Streams Component Ratio:** Remember the speed-to-time ratio shortcut for upstream/downstream distance-constancy questions: $\frac{\text{Boat Speed (v)}}{\text{Stream Speed (s)}} = \frac{t_{up} + t_{down}}{t_{up} - t_{down}}$. It solves complex word problems in a single line.
