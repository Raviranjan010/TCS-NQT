## Error Worked Examples
This file contains 5 worked examples mirroring the exact format of the TCS NQT verbal section. Each question contains a sentence split into parts, where one part contains a grammatical error, along with a detailed explanation of the correction rules applied.

---

## Example 1 (Subject-Verb Agreement)
**Identify the part of the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

"The design of the new database system, (A) / along with several security patches, (B) / were completed by the development team (C) / yesterday afternoon. (D)"

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Part A
    *   B. Part B
    *   C. Part C
    *   D. Part D
*   **Correct Answer:** C (Part C)
*   **Explanation:** 
    *   **Rule:** When a singular subject is followed by intervening parenthetical phrases starting with words like *along with*, *as well as*, *together with*, or *in addition to*, the subject remains singular. The verb must agree with the true subject, not the nouns in the intervening phrase.
    *   **Application:** The true subject is the singular noun "**design**" (in Part A). The plural phrase "along with several security patches" (in Part B) does not change the subject to plural. Therefore, the plural auxiliary verb "**were**" in Part C must be replaced with the singular auxiliary verb "**was**".
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "The design of the new database system, along with several security patches, **was** completed by the development team yesterday afternoon."

---

## Example 2 (Tenses)
**Identify the part of the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

"If the client will submit (A) / the feedback by tomorrow morning, (B) / we will easily complete the deployment (C) / before the weekend deadline. (D)"

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Part A
    *   B. Part B
    *   C. Part C
    *   D. Part D
*   **Correct Answer:** A (Part A)
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** In first conditional sentences, the conditional clause (the "if-clause") must be in the simple present tense, whereas the main clause must be in the simple future tense (using *will/shall*). We do not use the future auxiliary "will" in both clauses.
    *   **Application:** The conditional clause is "If the client will submit" (Part A), and the main clause is "we will easily complete the deployment" (Part C). To fix this, change "will submit" to the simple present form "**submits**" (matching the singular subject "client").
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "If the client **submits** the feedback by tomorrow morning, we will easily complete the deployment before the weekend deadline."

---

## Example 3 (Prepositions)
**Identify the part of the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

"The marketing committee (A) / met in the boardroom (B) / to discuss about the strategies (C) / for the upcoming product launch. (D)"

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Part A
    *   B. Part B
    *   C. Part C
    *   D. Part D
*   **Correct Answer:** C (Part C)
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** Transitive verbs take their object directly without a preposition. Using a preposition like "about" after verbs like *discuss*, *describe*, *order*, or *enter* (when entering a room) is redundant and grammatically incorrect.
    *   **Application:** The verb "**discuss**" in Part C is a transitive verb that directly takes the noun phrase "the strategies" as its object. Adding the preposition "about" is redundant.
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "The marketing committee met in the boardroom to **discuss the strategies** for the upcoming product launch."

---

## Example 4 (Articles)
**Identify the part of the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

"Our HR department (A) / conducted an online survey (B) / to find out if he is (C) / a honest employee. (D)"

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Part A
    *   B. Part B
    *   C. Part C
    *   D. Part D
*   **Correct Answer:** D (Part D)
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** The indefinite article *a* is used before words that begin with a consonant sound, while *an* is used before words that begin with a vowel sound. The spelling of the first letter does not matter; the focus is entirely on pronunciation.
    *   **Application:** The word "**honest**" starts with a silent letter 'h'. The initial sound is the vowel sound "/ɒ/" (similar to 'onest'). Thus, it requires the article "**an**" instead of "a".
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "Our HR department conducted an online survey to find out if he is **an** honest employee."

---

## Example 5 (Tense Consistency)
**Identify the part of the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

"Although the manager (A) / has been working in the company (B) / since ten years, (C) / he is still unfamiliar with the new ERP system. (D)"

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Part A
    *   B. Part B
    *   C. Part C
    *   D. Part D
*   **Correct Answer:** C (Part C)
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** When expressing the duration of an action in the perfect or perfect continuous tenses, use the preposition "**for**" to denote a period of time (e.g., ten years, five hours), and "**since**" to denote a specific starting point in time (e.g., 2015, Monday, morning).
    *   **Application:** In Part C, "ten years" refers to a duration/period of time, not a specific point. Therefore, the preposition "since" is incorrect and must be replaced with "**for**".
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "Although the manager has been working in the company **for** ten years, he is still unfamiliar with the new ERP system."

## Fill Worked Examples
This file contains 5 fully worked examples covering single-blank and double-blank formats, mirroring the questions in the TCS NQT verbal section. Each example includes a step-by-step analysis demonstrating how to eliminate options using grammar, tone, and logical connectors.

---

## Example 1 (Single-Blank: Contrast Transition)
**Select the most appropriate word to fill in the blank:**

"Despite the team's ________ efforts to resolve the software bug before the release, the launch had to be postponed by a week due to unresolved security patches."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. sporadic
    *   B. valiant
    *   C. superficial
    *   D. negligent
*   **Correct Answer:** B (valiant)
*   **Step-by-Step Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify the Logic:** The sentence starts with the contrast transition word "**Despite**". This indicates that the word in the blank should have an opposite tone/charge to the negative outcome (the launch being postponed due to unresolved patches).
    2.  **Determine Tone:** Since the outcome is negative ("postponed," "unresolved security patches"), the effort put in by the team must have been positive or intense.
    3.  **Evaluate Options:**
        *   *A. sporadic* (negative) - occurring at irregular intervals; scattered.
        *   *B. valiant* (positive) - possessing or showing courage or determination; heroic/brave.
        *   *C. superficial* (negative) - shallow, not thorough.
        *   *D. negligent* (negative) - failing to take proper care; careless.
    4.  **Conclusion:** "Valiant" is the only positive word that logically fits the context of strong efforts that unfortunately still resulted in postponement.
*   **Corrected Sentence:** "Despite the team's **valiant** efforts to resolve the software bug before the release, the launch had to be postponed..."

---

## Example 2 (Single-Blank: Contextual Vocabulary)
**Select the most appropriate word to fill in the blank:**

"The economic recession had a ________ effect on small businesses, causing many to shut down while others struggled to survive."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. salutary
    *   B. transient
    *   C. deleterious
    *   C. benign
*   **Correct Answer:** C (deleterious)
*   **Step-by-Step Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify the Logic:** The sentence describes the effect of an "economic recession" which caused businesses to "shut down" and "struggle."
    2.  **Determine Tone:** The context is highly negative. Therefore, the blank must be filled with a word representing a harmful or negative effect.
    3.  **Evaluate Options:**
        *   *A. salutary* (positive) - producing good effects; beneficial.
        *   *B. transient* (neutral) - lasting only for a short time; temporary.
        *   *C. deleterious* (negative) - causing harm or damage.
        *   *D. benign* (positive) - gentle, kindly, harmless.
    4.  **Conclusion:** "Deleterious" fits the negative context perfectly.
*   **Corrected Sentence:** "The economic recession had a **deleterious** effect on small businesses..."

---

## Example 3 (Double-Blank: Contrast Structure)
**Select the most appropriate pair of words to fill in the blanks:**

"Although the initial mock tests were ________, the final NQT exam proved to be highly ________, catching many candidates off guard."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. simple, elementary
    *   B. challenging, arduous
    *   C. trivial, demanding
    *   D. rigorous, painless
*   **Correct Answer:** C (trivial, demanding)
*   **Step-by-Step Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify the Logic:** The sentence uses the contrast conjunction "**Although**" at the start, signaling that the first blank and the second blank must contain words of opposing tones (opposite charges).
    2.  **Analyze Clues:** The final clause says the final exam caught candidates "off guard," suggesting it was difficult. Therefore, Blank 2 should represent "difficult" (negative/challenging), and Blank 1 should represent "easy" (positive/simple).
    3.  **Evaluate Options:**
        *   *A. simple (easy) / elementary (easy)* $\rightarrow$ Both are easy. (Eliminate: No contrast)
        *   *B. challenging (hard) / arduous (hard)* $\rightarrow$ Both are hard. (Eliminate: No contrast)
        *   *C. trivial (easy/unimportant) / demanding (hard)* $\rightarrow$ Strong contrast. Trivial is easy, demanding is difficult. (Keep)
        *   *D. rigorous (hard) / painless (easy)* $\rightarrow$ Contrasting, but in the wrong order. Blank 1 would mean hard and Blank 2 would mean easy, which contradicts "catching candidates off guard." (Eliminate)
    4.  **Conclusion:** Option C is the only logically sound combination.
*   **Corrected Sentence:** "Although the initial mock tests were **trivial**, the final NQT exam proved to be highly **demanding**, catching many candidates..."

---

## Example 4 (Double-Blank: Cause-Effect)
**Select the most appropriate pair of words to fill in the blanks:**

"The manager's ________ attitude during client negotiations ________ the team, leading to a breakdown in communication and a loss of trust."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. aggressive, alienated
    *   B. collaborative, discouraged
    *   C. diplomatic, agitated
    *   D. indifferent, motivated
*   **Correct Answer:** A (aggressive, alienated)
*   **Step-by-Step Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify the Logic:** The phrase "leading to a breakdown in communication and a loss of trust" indicates a cause-and-effect relationship where a negative attitude (Blank 1) led to a negative consequence for the team (Blank 2).
    2.  **Evaluate Options:**
        *   *A. aggressive (negative) / alienated (negative)* $\rightarrow$ An aggressive attitude would alienate (estrange) the team. This matches the breakdown in communication. (Keep)
        *   *B. collaborative (positive) / discouraged (negative)* $\rightarrow$ A collaborative attitude would not discourage a team. (Eliminate: Contradictory)
        *   *C. diplomatic (positive) / agitated (negative)* $\rightarrow$ A diplomatic attitude is tactful and generally leads to positive coordination, not agitation. (Eliminate)
        *   *D. indifferent (negative) / motivated (positive)* $\rightarrow$ An indifferent attitude would not motivate a team to lose trust. (Eliminate)
    3.  **Conclusion:** Option A fits both blanks logically and grammatically.
*   **Corrected Sentence:** "The manager's **aggressive** attitude during client negotiations **alienated** the team..."

---

## Example 5 (Double-Blank: Tone Harmony)
**Select the most appropriate pair of words to fill in the blanks:**

"To prevent unauthorized access, the system administrator implemented ________ encryption standards, thereby making the database virtually ________ to hackers."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. obsolete, vulnerable
    *   B. robust, impenetrable
    *   C. weak, accessible
    *   D. complex, susceptible
*   **Correct Answer:** B (robust, impenetrable)
*   **Step-by-Step Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify the Logic:** The sentence starts with the purpose: "To prevent unauthorized access." Therefore, the action (Blank 1) must be positive/strong, and the result (Blank 2) must be that the database is safe/secure.
    2.  **Determine Tone:** Blank 1 needs a positive descriptor for encryption (e.g., strong, robust, secure). Blank 2 needs a word that means "safe from" or "closed to" hackers (e.g., impenetrable, inaccessible).
    3.  **Evaluate Options:**
        *   *A. obsolete (negative) / vulnerable (negative)* $\rightarrow$ Obsolete encryption would make the database vulnerable, which fails to prevent unauthorized access. (Eliminate)
        *   *B. robust (positive) / impenetrable (positive safety)* $\rightarrow$ Robust encryption makes the database impenetrable (unbreakable) to hackers. This achieves the goal. (Keep)
        *   *C. weak (negative) / accessible (negative safety)* $\rightarrow$ Weak encryption would make it accessible, failing the goal. (Eliminate)
        *   *D. complex (positive) / susceptible (negative safety)* $\rightarrow$ Complex encryption would not make it susceptible (vulnerable) to hackers. (Eliminate: Contradictory)
    4.  **Conclusion:** Option B is the only combination that fulfills the sentence's objective.
*   **Corrected Sentence:** "To prevent unauthorized access, the system administrator implemented **robust** encryption standards, thereby making the database virtually **impenetrable** to hackers."

## Sentence Worked Examples
This file contains 5 worked examples of Sentence Improvement in the exact format used in the TCS NQT. Each question features a sentence with an underlined phrase that may be grammatically incorrect, followed by improvement options and a detailed explanation of the grammatical rules applied.

---

## Example 1 (Dangling Modifiers)
**Improve the underlined part of the sentence. If the sentence is correct, select "No improvement required".**

"<u>Having completed the coding phase</u>, the application was submitted by the developers for testing."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. Having completed the coding phase, the developers submitted the application
    *   B. After completing the coding phase, the application was submitted
    *   C. On completion of the coding phase, the application was submitted by the developers
    *   D. No improvement required
*   **Correct Answer:** A
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must modify the subject of the main clause. In the original sentence, "Having completed the coding phase" modifies "the application" (which cannot code). The subject of the main clause must be the logical actor: "the developers."
    *   **Option Evaluation:** Option A changes the passive clause so that "the developers" becomes the subject, resolving the dangling modifier.
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "**Having completed the coding phase, the developers submitted the application** for testing."

---

## Example 2 (Parallel Structure)
**Improve the underlined part of the sentence. If the sentence is correct, select "No improvement required".**

"The consultant's duties include analyzing business workflows, writing reports, and <u>to present recommendations to the stakeholders</u>."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. to present recommendations for the stakeholders
    *   B. presenting recommendations to the stakeholders
    *   C. presentation of recommendations to stakeholders
    *   D. No improvement required
*   **Correct Answer:** B
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** Parallelism dictates that elements in a series must have the same grammatical form. Here, the first two items are gerund phrases: *analyzing business workflows* and *writing reports*. The third item must also be a gerund phrase to maintain parallel structure.
    *   **Option Evaluation:** Option B converts the infinitive phrase "to present..." into the gerund phrase "**presenting**...", matching the preceding terms.
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "The consultant's duties include analyzing business workflows, writing reports, and **presenting recommendations to the stakeholders**."

---

## Example 3 (Redundancy)
**Improve the underlined part of the sentence. If the sentence is correct, select "No improvement required".**

"Due to a technical glitch, the system administrator had to <u>revert back to the previous version</u> of the database."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. revert to the previous version
    *   B. revert back again to the previous version
    *   C. reverse back to the previous version
    *   D. No improvement required
*   **Correct Answer:** A
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** Redundancy occurs when words with duplicate meanings are used together. The verb "revert" means to return to a previous state, practice, or topic. Adding the word "back" is redundant.
    *   **Option Evaluation:** Option A removes the word "back", leaving the sentence concise and grammatically correct.
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "Due to a technical glitch, the system administrator had to **revert to the previous version** of the database."

---

## Example 4 (Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement)
**Improve the underlined part of the sentence. If the sentence is correct, select "No improvement required".**

"Each of the project coordinators must submit <u>their status report</u> by Friday evening."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. there status report
    *   B. his or her status report
    *   C. their status reports
    *   D. No improvement required
*   **Correct Answer:** B
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** Singular indefinite pronouns like *each*, *every*, *everyone*, *someone*, and *nobody* require singular verbs and singular possessive pronouns (*his*, *her*, *his or her*) for formal agreement, despite the plural noun in the prepositional phrase ("of the project coordinators").
    *   **Option Evaluation:** "Each" is the singular subject, so the possessive pronoun must be singular: "**his or her**".
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "Each of the project coordinators must submit **his or her status report** by Friday evening."

## Example 5 (Tense Consistency)
**Improve the underlined part of the sentence. If the sentence is correct, select "No improvement required".**

"The senior engineer <u>had worked in the firm since five years</u> when the organization decided to upgrade its core technology stack."

*   **Options:**
    *   A. has worked in the firm since five years
    *   B. had been working in the firm for five years
    *   C. was working in the firm for five years
    *   D. No improvement required
*   **Correct Answer:** B
*   **Explanation:**
    *   **Rule:** When describing a continuous action that was ongoing in the past up to a certain point, use the Past Perfect Continuous tense (*had been working*). Additionally, the preposition "for" is used to show a duration of time ("five years"), not "since" (which marks a starting point).
    *   **Option Evaluation:** Option B correctly combines the past perfect continuous tense with the preposition "for" to represent the ongoing five-year period of work leading up to the technology upgrade.
    *   **Corrected Sentence:** "The senior engineer **had been working in the firm for five years** when the organization decided to upgrade its core technology stack."

## Para Worked Examples
This file contains 3 worked examples of Para Jumbles in the format used in the TCS NQT. Each example shows how to identify opening sentences, build mandatory pairs using pronouns and transition words, and arrive at the correct sequence step-by-step.

## Example 1 (Pronoun Reference Link)
**Arrange the following sentences in a logical order to form a coherent paragraph:**

*   **A.** Consequently, his designs laid the foundation for the modern multi-purpose computers we use today.
*   **B.** During the Second World War, he played a pivotal role in cracking intercepted coded messages.
*   **C.** Alan Turing was a British mathematician and logician who is widely considered the father of theoretical computer science.
*   **D.** He did this by designing the Bombe, an electromechanical machine that could decipher German Enigma codes.

*   **Options:**
    *   A. CBDA
    *   B. CABD
    *   C. CBAD
    *   D. BCDA
*   **Correct Answer:** A (CBDA)
*   **Step-by-Step Explanation:**
    1.  **Find the Opening Sentence:** 
        *   Sentence A starts with "Consequently," which is a conclusion transition word. It cannot open.
        *   Sentences B and D contain the pronouns "he" and "his" without introducing who "he" is.
        *   Sentence C introduces the full name "**Alan Turing**" and states who he was. This is an independent, introductory sentence. Therefore, **C** is the opening sentence.
    2.  **Identify Noun-Pronoun Links (Mandatory Pairs):**
        *   Sentence B says "During the Second World War, **he** played..." referring back to Alan Turing. So, C must precede B ($C \rightarrow B$).
        *   Sentence D says "**He** did **this** by designing..." "This" refers to "cracking intercepted coded messages" in Sentence B. Therefore, B and D form a mandatory pair: **BD**.
    3.  **Check the Closing Sentence:**
        *   Sentence A begins with the concluding transition "**Consequently**" and wraps up the paragraph by detailing the long-term impact of his designs. This belongs at the end.
    4.  **Assemble the Sequence:**
        *   Opening: C
        *   Link: B (war role) followed by D (how he did it) $\rightarrow$ BD
        *   Conclusion: A
        *   Resulting Order: **CBDA**

## Example 2 (Transition Word Link)
**Arrange the following sentences in a logical order to form a coherent paragraph:**

*   **A.** However, this centralized structure creates latency issues for real-time applications like self-driving cars.
*   **B.** Traditionally, smart city devices sent all their gathered data to central cloud databases.
*   **C.** Therefore, urban engineers are transitioning to localized edge computing nodes to reduce response delays.
*   **D.** These databases processed and analyzed the information before sending instructions back.

*   **Options:**
    *   A. BDAC
    *   B. BADC
    *   C. BACD
    *   D. ABDC
*   **Correct Answer:** A (BDAC)
*   **Step-by-Step Explanation:**
    1.  **Find the Opening Sentence:**
        *   Sentence A starts with the contrast transition "**However**".
        *   Sentence C starts with the conclusion transition "**Therefore**".
        *   Sentence D starts with the demonstrative pronoun phrase "**These databases**", implying databases were introduced in the previous sentence.
        *   Sentence B is a general historical statement: "Traditionally, smart city devices sent... to **central cloud databases**." This is our opening sentence.
    2.  **Establish Mandatory Pairs:**
        *   Sentence B introduces "central cloud databases". Sentence D starts with "These databases". This links B directly to D. Thus, **BD** is a mandatory pair.
        *   Sentence A starts with "However, this centralized structure..." which contrasts the traditional centralized structure described in B and D with its limitations (latency). So, D must be followed by A ($D \rightarrow A$).
        *   Sentence C starts with "Therefore," indicating the action taken to resolve the latency problem introduced in A. So, A must be followed by C ($A \rightarrow C$).
    3.  **Assemble the Sequence:**
        *   Starting with BD, followed by the contrast in A, and ending with the consequence/solution in C $\rightarrow$ **BDAC**.

## Active Worked Examples
This file contains 5 worked examples of Active and Passive Voice conversions. Each example features a detailed analysis of the subject, verb, and object, along with a step-by-step breakdown of how the sentence shifts using the standard tense conversion rules.

---

#| Q# | Tense | Active Voice | Subject (S) | Verb (V) | Object (O) | Passive Structure Applied | Passive Voice (Correct Answer) |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **1** | Simple Present | The manager conducts the weekly meeting. | The manager | conducts | the weekly meeting | O + is/am/are + V3 + by S | The weekly meeting **is conducted** by the manager. |
| **2** | Present Continuous | The developer is writing the database script. | The developer | is writing | the database script | O + is/am/are + being + V3 + by S | The database script **is being written** by the developer. |
| **3** | Simple Past | The committee approved the budget proposal. | The committee | approved | the budget proposal | O + was/were + V3 + by S | The budget proposal **was approved** by the committee. |
| **4** | Present Perfect | The QA team has tested the new release. | The QA team | has tested | the new release | O + has/have + been + V3 + by S | The new release **has been tested** by the QA team. |
| **5** | Imperative | Close the terminal window. | (You - implied) | Close | the terminal window | Let + O + be + V3 | **Let** the terminal window **be closed**. |

---

#### Example 1 (Simple Present Tense)
*   **Active:** "The manager conducts the weekly meeting."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify Subject & Object:** The subject is "The manager" (singular), and the object is "the weekly meeting" (singular).
    2.  **Identify Tense:** The verb "conducts" is in the Simple Present tense.
    3.  **Apply Rule:** In the passive voice, the auxiliary verb for a singular object in the Simple Present is "**is**", followed by the past participle (V3) of "conduct" $\rightarrow$ "**conducted**".
*   **Passive:** "The weekly meeting **is conducted** by the manager."

### Example 2 (Present Continuous Tense)
*   **Active:** "The developer is writing the database script."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify Subject & Object:** Subject is "The developer", object is "the database script".
    2.  **Identify Tense:** The verb "is writing" is in the Present Continuous tense.
    3.  **Apply Rule:** The continuous aspect requires adding "**being**" after the auxiliary verb. Since "the database script" is singular, we use "**is being**" + V3 of write $\rightarrow$ "**written**".
*   **Passive:** "The database script **is being written** by the developer."

### Example 3 (Simple Past Tense)
*   **Active:** "The committee approved the budget proposal."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify Subject & Object:** Subject is "The committee", object is "the budget proposal".
    2.  **Identify Tense:** The verb "approved" is in the Simple Past tense.
    3.  **Apply Rule:** For a singular object in the Simple Past, we use the auxiliary "**was**" + V3 of approve $\rightarrow$ "**approved**".
*   **Passive:** "The budget proposal **was approved** by the committee."

### Example 4 (Present Perfect Tense)
*   **Active:** "The QA team has tested the new release."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify Subject & Object:** Subject is "The QA team", object is "the new release".
    2.  **Identify Tense:** "has tested" is in the Present Perfect tense.
    3.  **Apply Rule:** The perfect aspect requires adding "**been**" after the auxiliary verb. Since "the new release" is singular, we use "**has been**" + V3 of test $\rightarrow$ "**tested**".
*   **Passive:** "The new release **has been tested** by the QA team."

### Example 5 (Imperative Sentence - Order/Command)
*   **Active:** "Close the terminal window."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Identify Structure:** This is an imperative sentence expressing a command, with the subject "you" implied.
    2.  **Apply Rule:** The formula for an imperative command in the passive voice is: `Let + Object + be + Past Participle (V3)`.
    3.  **Application:** The object is "the terminal window", and the verb is "close" (V3 is "closed").
*   **Passive:** "**Let** the terminal window **be closed**."

## Direct Worked Examples
This file contains 5 worked examples of Direct to Indirect (Reported) Speech conversions. Each example includes a step-by-step breakdown of tense shifts, pronoun changes, and time/place marker updates.

---

#| Q# | Sentence Type | Direct Speech | Reporting Verb | Pronoun Shift (SON Rule) | Tense Backshift | Place/Time Marker Shift | Indirect Speech (Correct Answer) |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **1** | Assertive | The developer said, "I **am writing** a new script **today**." | said (Past) | I $\rightarrow$ he | Present Continuous $\rightarrow$ Past Continuous | today $\rightarrow$ that day | The developer said that he **was writing** a new script **that day**. |
| **2** | Assertive | She said to me, "We **have completed** the design." | said to $\rightarrow$ told | We $\rightarrow$ they | Present Perfect $\rightarrow$ Past Perfect | None | She told me that they **had completed** the design. |
| **3** | Interrogative | The lead engineer asked, "Are **you** coming **tomorrow**?" | asked (Past) | you $\rightarrow$ I/he/they (contextual) | Present Continuous $\rightarrow$ Past Continuous | tomorrow $\rightarrow$ the next day | The lead engineer asked if I **was coming the next day**. |
| **4** | Interrogative (Wh-) | He asked, "**Where** **did** you buy this laptop?" | asked (Past) | you $\rightarrow$ I | Simple Past $\rightarrow$ Past Perfect | this $\rightarrow$ that | He asked where I **had bought that** laptop. |
| **5** | Imperative | The director said, "**Please** submit the reports." | said $\rightarrow$ requested | None (implied you) | Infinitive (to + V1) applied | None | The director requested me **to submit** the reports. |

---

#### Example 1 (Assertive Sentence with Time Shift)
*   **Direct:** The developer said, "I **am writing** a new script **today**."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Reporting Verb:** "said" is in the past tense, so we must backshift the tenses and time markers in the quotation.
    2.  **Pronoun Shift:** The first-person pronoun "**I**" refers to "The developer" (third person singular, male/female). We shift it to "**he**" (or "she").
    3.  **Tense Backshift:** The Present Continuous "**am writing**" shifts to the Past Continuous "**was writing**".
    4.  **Time Marker Shift:** The time word "**today**" shifts to "**that day**".
*   **Indirect:** The developer said that he **was writing** a new script **that day**.

### Example 2 (Assertive Sentence with "said to")
*   **Direct:** She said to me, "We **have completed** the design."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Reporting Verb:** "said to" followed by an object ("me") changes to "**told**".
    2.  **Pronoun Shift:** The first-person plural "**We**" shifts to the third-person plural "**they**" (representing the group she belongs to).
    3.  **Tense Backshift:** The Present Perfect "**have completed**" shifts to the Past Perfect "**had completed**".
*   **Indirect:** She told me that they **had completed** the design.

### Example 3 (Interrogative Sentence - Yes/No Question)
*   **Direct:** The lead engineer asked, "Are **you** coming **tomorrow**?"
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Sentence Type:** This is a Yes/No question, so we use the conjunction "**if**" or "**whether**" to connect the clauses. The reporting verb "**asked**" remains.
    2.  **Pronoun Shift:** The second-person pronoun "**you**" refers to the listener (assumed first-person "**I**" here).
    3.  **Tense Backshift:** The interrogative clause structure changes to assertive, and the tense shifts from Present Continuous ("Are you coming") to Past Continuous ("**I was coming**").
    4.  **Time Marker Shift:** The word "**tomorrow**" shifts to "**the next day**" or "the following day".
*   **Indirect:** The lead engineer asked if I **was coming the next day**.

### Example 4 (Interrogative Sentence - Wh- Question)
*   **Direct:** He asked, "**Where** **did** you buy this laptop?"
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Sentence Type:** This is a "Wh-" question, so the question word "**where**" acts as the connector. We do not use "that" or "if".
    2.  **Pronoun Shift:** "**you**" shifts to "**I**".
    3.  **Tense Backshift:** The question is in the Simple Past ("did buy"), which shifts to the Past Perfect "**had bought**".
    4.  **Place Marker Shift:** "**this**" shifts to "**that**".
*   **Indirect:** He asked where I **had bought that** laptop.

### Example 5 (Imperative Sentence - Request)
*   **Direct:** The director said, "**Please** submit the reports."
*   **Analysis:**
    1.  **Sentence Type:** The word "Please" identifies this as a request. We change the reporting verb "said" to "**requested**".
    2.  **Infinitive Link:** Connect the reported clause using an infinitive: "**to**" + base verb ("**submit**").
*   **Indirect:** The director requested me **to submit** the reports.
